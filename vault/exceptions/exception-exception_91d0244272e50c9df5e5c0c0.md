LIMITATIONS\nUR-VC assumes that visually similar observations indicate\nsimilar task progress. This assumption is shared with progress\nestimator and value models: when visually similar states occur\nat different task stages or after different action histories, their\nlatent progress can be ambiguous. Episode-balanced averaging\nreduces trajectory-specific timing noise, but it cannot correct\nsystematic retrieval errors.\nOur downstream evaluation focuses on one representative\nuse case: using UR-VC-derived supervision for advantage-\nconditioned VLA training in real bimanual cloth manipulation.\nUnder matched experimental settings, the corrected labels lead\nto higher average success, suggesting their practical utility as\na drop-in supervision signal. Future work may further examine\nthe method across broader task families, training scales, and\nhyperparameter settings.\nVI. CONCLUSION\nUR-VC corrects time-derived progress proxies by averaging\nsemantic state recurrences across independent robot episodes.\nIt exploits dataset redundancy to better align scalable proxy\nlabels with latent physical progress. On real bimanual cloth\ndata, cross-episode matches are abundant and semantic, cor-\nrected estimates recover regressions that a monotone time\nproxy cannot express, and the resulting signal can be used\nfor advantage-conditioned VLA training. Time provides useful\nand scalable supervision, but it should not be conflated with\nphysical progress.
